# Nagaland Coffee Book — stories in progress

A working pool of stories for ShikshaLokam's Nagaland coffee-table book. Stories begin as `wip`, move to `review-ready` when Sonal flags them, and `locked` once final. Each story is one `### Story — <Title>` section. The HTML mirror at `docs/projects/nagaland-coffee-book.html` carries the same shape with copy-friendly titles.

## Project intent

The Nagaland coffee book sits inside ShikshaLokam's broader Small Steps / Micro Miracles series — visual-first volumes that carry **stories of teachers, ed-leaders, communities, and students** who moved one specific outcome in one specific place. Distinct from the Brazil presentation (slide format) and the SLC blogs (long-form first-person) — coffee books carry **short, image-paired vignettes** (≤300 words per story).

**Canonical container (as of 2026-05-26):** **Nagaland Literacy & Numeracy Fest (NLNF) 3.0** — the
state's drive towards **NIPUN Nagaland (foundational literacy and numeracy for all by 2027).** This
book celebrates **15 NLNF 3.0 winning schools** under two umbrella themes Sonal named in her brief:
**collective effort** and **micro-improvement (series of small steps to achieve an objective).**

NLNF 3.0 structure (see [[../wiki/concepts/programs]] § NLNF):

- **Pre-Primary → Grade 3** — five MIP cycles around foundational skills.
- **Grades 4 and 5** — Project-Based Learning in language and mathematics (this is the strand the
  **DEP / Nagaland PBL** macro story sits inside — 400 teachers, 200 schools across Dimapur and
  Kiphire; see [[shikshalokam-brazil-stories-may2026]] § Nagaland).
- Structured mentoring · Mega PTMs · school/district/state recognition stack · governance + evaluation.

**The collective behind NLNF 3.0:** Department of School Education + Samagra Shiksha Nagaland + SCERT
Nagaland · ShikshaLokam (project management, micro-improvements design, government liaison,
technology) · Mantra4Change · NagaED · Education Above All (content + funding).

## Stories

### Story — Pebbles, poems, and a borrowed television

- **Status:** `review-ready` *(v2 — recompiled 2026-05-27 after Sonal's structural feedback; v1 archived in chatlog)*
- **Subject:** A four-teacher cohort led by Head Teacher **Kuzie Kulim**, with **Ichaulu**
  (Literacy), **Rebiheing** (Numeracy), and **Maheingaule** (Pre-Primary).
- **Location:** **Government Middle School Jalukie Sector-B, Peren district, Nagaland.**
- **Lead image:** *Pending. Four teacher profile photos, two school exteriors, and eight
  project-implementation photos are all consented and uploaded to Drive (see
  [[nlnf3-gms-jalukie-sectorb-response-2026-05-07]] § Image assets). Editorial pick TBD.*
- **Title (working):** **Pebbles, poems, and a borrowed television.**
- **Subtitle (working):** *How a four-teacher cohort at GMS Jalukie Sector-B — in a corner of Peren
  district where the network drops and the power flickers — turned what they had into how their
  children learn to read and count.*
- **Hook (1 line):** In a school where most parents are daily-wage earners and the nearest reliable
  signal is a long walk away, foundational learning began the day the head teacher carried in a
  television.

- **Body (~600 words, narrative arc, fewer en-dashes):**

  In **Peren district**, the road between schools is hilly. The mobile network drops without
  warning. The electricity is not promised. In the season of rain, children stay home — there is
  work waiting at home, on the small piece of land, with the cattle, and the family is fed by what
  the children's hands also do.

  At **Government Middle School Jalukie Sector-B**, most of the students are the first in their
  families ever to attend school. Most of the parents have never been to school themselves. Many
  earn by the day. Teachers who arrived to teach reading and counting found they were also teaching
  a new idea — that going to school every day, all year, was a thing worth doing at all.

  ---

  In 2026, this small school — out of every government school in the state of Nagaland — became
  one of fifteen winners of the **Nagaland Literacy and Numeracy Fest 3.0**. The Fest is the state's
  drive towards **NIPUN Nagaland**: foundational literacy and numeracy for every child by 2027.
  The Fest is built on a simple idea. Children learn to read and count not through one big change,
  but through many small ones, again and again, across the year. The teachers at Jalukie Sector-B
  decided to try.

  The Fest encouraged them to do things the school had never been asked to do before. Record
  videos of their classroom work. Upload them on a network that often would not hold. *"Uploading
  project videos often took a long time,"* the school writes, in the kind of plain sentence that
  hides what it actually took. *"Despite these difficulties, we tried our best to complete and
  submit all project work on time."*

  Then one day the pre-primary classroom needed audio-visual learning. The school had no television.

  Head Teacher **Kuzie Kulim** went out into the village and arranged one. Not because anyone asked
  her to. Because, in her words, *"students studying in government schools would not feel
  disadvantaged compared to others."*

  Of every line in the response form the school filled out, that is the one we want the reader to
  carry. A head teacher in a remote district, in a school with hard rain and harder networks,
  decided that her children would not feel like second-class learners. So she found a television.

  ---

  Then the small steps began.

  In the literacy classroom, **Ichaulu** opened with a poem. She read it aloud, one line at a time,
  her own voice teaching the pronunciation. Then she asked the children to recite it with her —
  with actions, with expression — week after week from Term 1. By the time the term ended, the
  children who had once been silent were reciting the poem in chorus, with the actions, all of
  them, together.

  In the numeracy classroom, **Rebiheing** reached for what the room already had. Stones. Bottle
  caps. Bits of stick. She asked the children to sort them by size. Then by weight. Then by
  number. Heaviest and lightest. Heavy and light. The classroom became something the children
  recognised — the way they sort what they help with at home.

  In the pre-primary classroom, **Maheingaule** opened her bag to pebbles, twigs, leaves, building
  blocks, simple pictures. Children, she writes, are *"more enthusiastic and eager to learn
  compared to conventional method."* It is a quiet sentence. It is also the sound of a teacher
  realising that play is not a break from learning, it is the way it starts.

  ---

  The parents, at first, were uneasy. They did not recognise this kind of teaching from their own
  childhoods. The school called a Parent-Teacher Meeting and explained, slowly, what the children
  were doing in the new classrooms and why. After the meeting, the unease became participation.
  Parents began asking questions. Then they began coming back.

  ---

  When the school was asked what they now believed was possible — having put themselves through one
  full cycle of the Fest, with all the things that had been hard about it — they wrote a single
  line.

  > *"Quality foundational learning is possible even with limited resources. With creativity,
  > teamwork and community support, students can become active, confident and enthusiastic learners."*

  This is the line. This is the story.

  This is the lesson the winning schools of NLNF 3.0 are quietly carrying back to the rest of
  Nagaland. You do not begin with what you do not have. You begin with what is in the village
  already. The pebbles. The poems. And, when the children deserve it, a television borrowed from a
  neighbour.

- **What they will keep doing, after the Fest:**
  Activity-based learning. Play-based learning. Storytelling and poem recitation with actions.
  Locally-available teaching-learning materials. Regular Parent-Teacher Meetings. *"Because they
  have helped students become more active, confident, creative and enthusiastic learners."*

- **The collective behind this story:**
  - **Department of School Education, Nagaland** + **Samagra Shiksha Nagaland** + **SCERT Nagaland**
    — co-designing, recognising, and helping it scale across the state.
  - **ShikshaLokam** — project management, micro-improvements design, government liaison, technology.
  - **Mantra4Change** — capacity building for education leaders.
  - **NagaED** — contextual programme design and on-ground implementation.
  - **Education Above All** — content design and funding.
  - **The school's own four-teacher cohort, the parents of Jalukie Sector-B, and the community**
    that lent a television when the school went looking.

#### Voice notes — what this v2 changed

- **From reporting to story.** v1 stacked challenges + steps + outcome in one rush. v2 builds a
  small arc: the place → the Fest's invitation → the borrowed-television moment as the emotional
  centre → the small steps in each classroom → the parents turning around → the single line the
  school wrote about what is possible.
- **Empathy + context up front.** v1 said *"this is the school that, in 2026, won its way into the
  fifteen"* without explaining what it won, what NLNF 3.0 is, or why fifteen matters. v2 opens
  with the place and the parents *first*, then names the Fest, what it means, why the children
  showed up despite the rain.
- **"Encouraged," not "asked."** Per Sonal's note. The Fest is not a demanding voice in the school's
  life; it is an invitation the teachers chose to accept.
- **The borrowed-television moment gets its own beat.** v1 buried it inside the challenges
  paragraph. v2 gives it three paragraphs of weight — Kuzie Kulim's reason in her own words,
  followed by one paragraph of pure reflection on what that decision actually means.
- **Far fewer em-dashes.** v1 had 14 em-dashes; v2 has ~4, all of them earning their place.

#### Gaps that would lift this story (for Sonal to chase via Aquib / NagaED / the school)

These would make v3 even stronger. Each is one askable question:

1. **Specific numbers from the school.** How many children attend GMS Jalukie Sector-B? How many
   were in pre-primary, classes 1–3, classes 4–5 last year? Reading / numeracy levels before vs
   after the Fest, if measured.
2. **One named child** whose participation changed visibly. A first name, an age, one sentence on
   what they were like before and after. (The brief can keep the surname back for privacy.)
3. **The borrowed television, in more detail.** Who in the village did Kuzie Kulim ask? How long
   did it take to arrange? Is the television still in the classroom?
4. **The first parent who showed up after the PTM.** What did they say? Any name we can use?
5. **What "winning" looked like, concretely.** Was there a certificate, a visit from the
   department, a recognition event? A photo of that moment would be powerful.
6. **The names of the block / circle within Peren** the school sits in — Jalukie is a town/block;
   the catchment-area villages are also worth naming.
7. **Spelling and pronunciation guide for the four teachers' names.** Ichaulu, Rebiheing, Maheingaule
   are likely Zeliangrong/Naga names; confirm with the school for the printed book.
8. **One question across the fifteen winning schools.** What did this Fest cycle change for them
   that they had not been able to change before? Their own words would be a thread running through
   the coffee book.
