## Portraits

### Portrait — Shri Vempuluru Nagoor Masthanaiah

- **Status:** `review-ready`
- **Role:** Recently retired Director, Commissioner of School Education (CSE), Samagra Shiksha,
  Andhra Pradesh. Forty years in service.
- **Location:** Origin village on the railway line between Gudur and Renigunta, Tirupati district,
  Andhra Pradesh. Served across Hyderabad, Medak, Nalgonda, and at the AP state HQ.
- **Lead image:** *Pending — Masthanaiah Sir has agreed to share photos from his career; team to chase before publication.*
- **Hook (1 line):**
  > When the interviewer summarises his journey as *from teacher to commissioner*, he cuts in:
  > *"Not from the teacher — from dropout of fifth class."*

- **Title (working):** **From the fifth-class dropout — forty years of holding the line.**
- **Subtitle (working):** *How Shri Vempuluru Nagoor Masthanaiah carried discipline from a village
  under a tree to Andhra Pradesh's longest-serving Commissioner of School Education.*

- **Body (~340 words):**

  In the village where Vempuluru Nagoor Masthanaiah began primary school, the school had no walls.
  *"It's just a small name tree under the tree in the street only,"* he says. Four classes ran in one
  room. Lamps were tonic-syrup bottles stuffed with a kerosene wick, the oil rationed by his father.
  He dropped out twice — first after fifth class, again after seventh. Each time, an outsider pulled
  him back. First his primary teacher, **Sankara Raju**, who walked two kilometres each day from the
  next village and is still the reason the village has any 10th-pass at all. Then a distant relative,
  a diploma engineer, who took him to **Gudur** and got him into a Scheduled Tribe hostel for ninth
  and tenth class. In **1979**, fifteen years after the school was founded, Nagoor became the first
  student it ever produced who passed tenth class. He has not stopped since.

  Forty years in **Andhra Pradesh's school education system** followed. Nine years as a teacher of
  Science and Mathematics for Classes 6 to 10 — *single-handed across three to four batches* — in a
  remote Nellore-district school. He bet on a simple rule: *"On the basis of strength enrollment of
  the students, the other facilities will come."* He turned two government upper-primaries into high
  schools. As Deputy Educational Officer in **Hyderabad** from 1993, he reformed the morning-afternoon
  shift system that had hollowed teaching hours, and — in 1996 — **originated the counselling-based
  teacher transfer system** that ended decades of pull-driven postings and is now standard practice
  across departments and states. *"Some pressure groups have suffered,"* he notes, with no apology.
  In his final 5.5 years as **Director, CSE Andhra Pradesh** — the longest tenure anyone has held
  that cadre post — he co-led the **SALT** project document for the World Bank, delivered the
  **second-highest number of PM SHRI schools** in India for AP (after Uttar Pradesh), and built the
  **School Leadership Development Programme**: 250 school heads to **IIM Ahmedabad**, then a cascade
  across all leaders of AP's ~45,000 schools, in a profession he had watched receive every form of
  training except the one for leadership.

- **Forward-looking close (1 line, in his own words):**
  > *"Aims must be good. Aims must be good. If aims are good, results automatically will be good."*

- **Attribution / consent:** *Verbal — agreed during interview to ShikshaLokam's use of his story
  for the Ed-Leader Portraits series. Photos pending separate share.*
- **Source thread:** Aquib Rizwan's interview, **20 April 2026** (1h 04m video call) — ingested as
  [[interview-masthanaiah-2026-04-20]]. Routed to brain on **26 May 2026** via Sonal's email
  "Content Creation with Brain."

#### Voice notes — what this portrait kept and resisted

- *Kept:* leader-foregrounded opening, location-rooted, one specific origin moment (the lamp from a
  tonic-syrup bottle), the named first teacher (Sankara Raju), the one-line own-voice close. Matches
  the live gallery's register — *"Holding Space for Every Learner" / "Turning a Failing School Around"* —
  descriptive arc-titles, not promotional taglines.
- *Resisted:* dressing him in language he didn't use. His English is broken, declarative, repetitive;
  his authority comes from concrete acts, not aphorisms. No Sanskrit-flourish, no "thought leader,"
  no superlatives.
- *Held:* the verb count. *Originated · reformed · delivered · cascaded.* He's a man who built systems;
  the verbs do the work.
