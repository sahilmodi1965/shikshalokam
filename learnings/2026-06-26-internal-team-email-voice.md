# Internal team-announcement email voice — what Sonal's edits taught the brain

The brain drafted a team-wide announcement (Mantra + ShikshaLokam) introducing the SL Brain.
Sonal edited the draft in Gmail; the brain read it back via the new `gs.py draft-read` and
diffed intent vs. final. Three durable signals for **internal team comms** (distinct from
external stakeholder/social voice):

1. **Open warmer and more relational.** "Hi all," → **"Dear team,"**, and add a check-in line —
   **"Hope you are doing well."** (Echoes the canonical newsletter opener "Hope this email finds
   you in good spirits" — the warm check-in carries into internal mail too.)
2. **Name the thing plainly up front.** Sonal added an explicit framing line:
   *"we've been bringing AI into our work to build the efficiency of the team."* For an internal
   announcement she wants the literal **what** (AI, team efficiency) stated, not only the softer
   "a new way of working." Lead with the concrete capability, then the brand name.
3. **Soften clever punchiness.** "Quick one — and an exciting one." → *"Here's a quick update, an
   exciting one at that."* The em-dash one-liner reads as a touch performative for team mail;
   she prefers a plainer, friendlier cadence.

Unchanged at this stage: the **"What we've automated so far"** bulleted pointers,
the "single source of truth" framing, and the "it gets smarter every time one of us uses it" close.

## The decisive signal: honest experience over selling AI (2026-06-29 rewrite)

Sonal rewrote the same announcement a third time, with the explicit note that the brain's version
"felt like I was selling AI rather than sharing an honest experience." This is the strongest,
most durable voice rule for internal team mail. Her edits, read back via `gs.py draft-read`:

1. **Field note, not announcement.** The piece is a person sharing what they tried — not a product
   launch. Cut every pitch line: "redesign how the work gets done," "What we've automated so far,"
   "this isn't a tool we hand over and forget." If a sentence sounds like a press release, it's wrong.
2. **Lead with substance, not shine.** She reordered SL Brain ("one place for everything") from the
   climax to the *top*, and put "drafts in our voice" second. The honest foundation leads; the
   impressive-sounding capability follows.
3. **Name the real tool.** "AI" → **"Claude Code"**. Concrete and grounded beats buzzy and abstract.
4. **Own the rough edges.** "still being built everyday," "the messy early weeks," "figuring out
   where it helps and where it doesn't." Admitted limitations are what make it experience, not sell.
   A clean all-wins list reads as marketing.
5. **First-person and warm.** "the part I was most eager about," a weekend hello, smileys. Sonal,
   not a comms department.
6. **Credit people, not the tech.** A genuine shout-out (Sahil Modi, "mentoring us through the messy
   early weeks") reframes the whole thing as *what we learned together, with help* — the opposite of
   "look what AI can do."
7. **Lived > listed.** She cut the brain's added feature-bullets (research, scheduling) and kept only
   the workflows she has actually used. Don't pad an experience note with a capability catalogue.
8. **Softer close.** "Would love to discuss this in depth" over "send me your thoughts/concerns."

**How to apply:** for any internal team mail from Sonal, write as an honest practitioner sharing a
real experience — warm first-person open, name the actual tool, lead with the substance, keep only
workflows actually lived, own what's still rough, credit the people who helped, and close by inviting
a conversation. Never frame it as a launch or a feature list; if it sounds like selling, rewrite it.
