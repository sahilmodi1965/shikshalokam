# Learning — 2026-06-08 — Portrait: diplomatic tone, system-change through-line, no invented scenes

First review pass from **Aquib Rizwan**, who creates SL content daily and was looped onto the brain
to help with enrichment. The feedback is craft-level and it sharpens the long-form portrait register
beyond what [[2026-05-27-story-over-report]] established. It is structural: it changes how *every*
portrait should be written, not just this one.

## What it means (the durable rules)

1. **A portrait opens with a hook paragraph, not a setup line.** The first paragraph must state the
   thesis of the person and earn the reader's attention before any chronology begins. Landing the
   subject's own defining line (here: *"Not from the teacher. From dropout of fifth class."*) inside
   that hook is stronger than opening on the mechanics of the interview.

2. **One through-line, threaded — not a list of beats.** A portrait of an education leader must carry
   *the argument about how leadership changes the system* from first paragraph to last. Each episode
   advances the same thread (here: *refuse to leave anyone out* → get the children in → open up
   transfers → train the leaders the system forgot). Transitions should name the escalation, so the
   reader feels the story move forward rather than restart each section.

3. **Diplomatic tone.** Conviction without combativeness. Show the leader holding a line *and* hearing
   people out. Avoid framing colleagues, unions, or seniors as villains; let the integrity of the
   decision carry the weight.

4. **Never depict a scene that did not happen.** No invented tableaux for effect. "The room watched
   the room" read well but described a moment the transcript never recorded — it was cut. If it isn't
   in the source, it isn't in the portrait.

5. **Cross-verify every instance against the transcript before it ships.** "He turned around and never
   paid those fees" had fused two separate true events into one dramatized false one (the mid-walk
   turnaround was an **MSc → B.Ed** switch; the teacher selection happened *during* his MSc first year).
   Drama is never worth a fact. When a vivid detail can't be pinned to the source, cut it or caveat it
   — the same call that removed the *"DEO Masthan"* nickname and the unverified "he suspended the third
   head" claim.

6. **Connect instances; smooth the paragraph seams.** Quotes must be introduced and land inside the
   prose, with connective tissue carrying the narrative across paragraph breaks. Hard `---` chapter
   rules inside a portrait body fragment the read — prefer transitional sentences.

> **Long-form Ed-Leader Portrait register.** Beyond the caption conventions above, the long-form
> portrait (the ~600–800-word profile, companion to the blog) follows six rules, set with the team
> during the Masthanaiah portrait:
> 1. **Open on a hook paragraph** that states who the person is and why they matter — ideally landing
>    their own defining line — before any chronology.
> 2. **Thread one through-line:** how this leader changed the system. Every beat advances the same
>    argument; transitions name the escalation so the story moves forward, never restarts.
> 3. **Diplomatic tone:** conviction without villains. Show the leader holding the line *and* hearing
>    people out.
> 4. **No invented scenes.** If a depiction isn't in the source, it doesn't go in the portrait.
> 5. **Cross-verify every instance** against the transcript; never fuse two true events into one
>    dramatized false one, and cut any vivid detail that can't be pinned to the source.
> 6. **Connect the instances:** introduce and land each quote inside the prose; smooth paragraph seams
>    with transitions rather than hard section breaks.
