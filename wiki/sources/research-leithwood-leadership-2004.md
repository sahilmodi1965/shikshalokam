# How Leadership Influences Student Learning — Leithwood et al. (2004)

**Full citation:** Leithwood, K., Louis, K. S., Anderson, S., & Wahlstrom, K. (2004).
*How Leadership Influences Student Learning: A Review of Research for the Learning
from Leadership Project.* New York: The Wallace Foundation.

**Trust tier:** Tier 1 — a foundation-commissioned review of research, widely cited
across two decades of education-leadership scholarship.

**URL:** https://wallacefoundation.org/report/how-leadership-influences-student-learning
**Verified:** 2026-05-21 via the `shikshalokam-research` skill (WebSearch — confirmed
authorship, publisher, and the canonical finding across multiple academic indices).

## The canonical finding (cite verbatim)

> **Leadership is second only to classroom instruction among all school-related
> factors that contribute to what students learn at school.**

This is the landmark, most-quoted finding of the review. It is the primary evidence
anchor for ShikshaLokam's thesis that the school leader is the highest-leverage point
in the system after the teacher.

## SL-messaging angle

**Education leadership / agency.** This study is the answer to *"why focus on the
leader?"* — Shikshagraha begins with the education leader, and this is the research
that says that is where the leverage is. Use it whenever the messaging argues for
investing in school leadership rather than curriculum-first reform.

## How to cite it in content

- In a caption / post: attribute as *"a landmark Wallace Foundation review of
  research"* or *"two decades of education-leadership research"* — the finding has
  held up since 2004 and is the origin of the "second only to teaching" claim.
- Do **not** attribute this specific claim to the OECD. OECD's *Improving School
  Leadership* (Pont, Nusche & Moorman, 2008) echoes it, but the precise finding
  originates here. (This corrects the placeholder citation on captions Variant 2.)
- One study framing: this is a *review of research*, so "research has consistently
  found" is accurate — stronger than a single-study hedge.
