# Voice

How ShikshaLokam sounds in its own materials. Pulled from the homepage, welcomeaboard subdomain, a
long-form blog post by Khushboo Awasthi (Co-Founder & COO), and a year of Sonal's LinkedIn captions.

## Distinctive register

**Aspirational, but pragmatic.** Refuses heroism. Foregrounds incremental, unglamorous work as the actual unit of leadership.

> Leadership Isn't About Titles – It's About the Act of Improvement
— [shikshalokam.org](https://shikshalokam.org/)

> Every School Transformation Story Begins with a Leader
— [shikshalokam.org](https://shikshalokam.org/)

> small, incremental changes or improvements...often made quickly with minimal resources
— [welcomeaboard.shikshalokam.org](https://welcomeaboard.shikshalokam.org/)

**Inclusive "we"; rhetorical questions.** Talks to readers as fellow practitioners, not subjects.

> Do you know of a great school without an able and efficient leader? Impossible!
— [welcomeaboard.shikshalokam.org](https://welcomeaboard.shikshalokam.org/)

**Conversational openings; everyday metaphor.** A long blog post by the COO compares leadership to "living in Goa" — sustained metaphor, conversational, unafraid of the mundane.

> Leadership roles are enticing... we often have a romanticized view of what it entails... leadership also involves handling unglamorous responsibilities such as performance reviews
— Khushboo Awasthi, [Leading Is Like Living In Goa](https://shikshalokam.org/leading-is-like-living-in-goa/)

> Just as living in Goa requires resilience and adaptability, so does leadership
— Khushboo Awasthi, same piece

## Recurring vocabulary

- **"Leaders" / "leadership"** — load-bearing words; almost always pluralised, almost always inclusive of system, community, youth, women
- **"Micro-improvements"** — house term; signals theory of change
- **"Ecosystem"** — used non-technically, to mean the field of actors
- **"People-Powered Movement"** / "people's movement" — Shikshagraha framing
- **"Sense, Make Sense, Learn, Improve"** — operating cycle, used as titling
- **"Catalyst"** — self-description (Education Leadership Catalyst)

## What it avoids

- Top-down language ("mandate," "rollout," "deliver to")
- Hero narratives (one teacher / one school as exception)
- Technocratic platform-talk — ELEVATE/DIKSHA are framed by what leaders do with them, not the tech itself

## Long-form Ed-Leader Portrait register

The long-form portrait — the ~600–800-word leader profile, companion to the blog and distinct from
the caption — has its own rules, set with the team while building the Masthanaiah portrait (Sonal's
[[../../learnings/2026-05-27-story-over-report]], then Aquib Rizwan's
[[../../learnings/2026-06-08-portrait-diplomatic-system-thread]]):

1. **Open on a hook paragraph** that states who the person is and why they matter — ideally landing
   their own defining line — *before* any chronology. Don't open on the mechanics of the interview.
2. **Thread one through-line:** how this leader changed the system. Every beat advances the same
   argument; transitions name the escalation so the story moves forward and never restarts. A portrait
   reads as a story, not a list of clubbed-together achievements.
3. **Diplomatic tone:** conviction without villains. Show the leader holding the line *and* hearing
   people out; let the integrity of the decision carry the weight, not the framing of opponents.
4. **No invented scenes.** If a depiction isn't in the source, it doesn't go in the portrait
   (the cut line "the room watched the room" is the cautionary example).
5. **Cross-verify every instance** against the transcript; never fuse two true events into one
   dramatized false one, and cut any vivid detail that can't be pinned to the source.
6. **Connect the instances:** introduce and land each quote inside the prose, and smooth paragraph
   seams with transitions rather than hard section breaks.

Still true here: third-person, leader-foregrounded, location-rooted opening, named role + organisation,
and a forward-looking close in the leader's own words.

## Caption form (social, primarily LinkedIn)

How SL sounds in social captions specifically. Derived from a year of captions Sonal authored for ShikshaLokam — Jun 2025 → Mar 2026, ~150–180 distinct posts across Ed-Leader Portraits, studio teasers, event recaps, welcomes, polls, and hiring posts. Source: [SM Captions 2025-26](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1CBg6cYWPg-_r-HjU6ZCkjoRVYld6gpo_SKqN68wwEeQ/edit). Captions share the broader register above but layer specific conventions. Provided by Sonal 2026-05-13; treat as user-sourced.

### Opening moves

Captions overwhelmingly open with either a rhetorical question or a one-line aphorism placed as its own paragraph. Question-openers dominate — "What does it take to…" recurs ~15 times verbatim across the year.

> What does it take to bring a student back from the edge of dropping out?
— Ed-Leader Portrait, "SM Captions 2025-26"

> A forest does not grow because of one mighty tree. It thrives because everything is connected.
— InvokED 5.0 Leadership Spotlight recap, same source

Studio teasers (Up Close & Personal) open differently — with a pulled quote in curly quotes, attributed via em-dash to a named person and title.

> "Leadership is about enabling others to see the best that is possible that they can aspire to and supporting them in actually getting there." — @Subir Shukla, Former Educational Quality Improvement Advisor, GoI.
— Up Close & Personal teaser, same source

### Cadence

Two registers coexist within a single caption. Short stacked sentences separated by empty-line breaks carry hooks and reflections. Longer, comma- and em-dash-heavy paragraphs carry the context block in leader portraits ("This was the reality X walked into…"). Studio posts canonically use three bullets to outline an episode.

### Pronouns

"We" / "us" / "our" dominate and read as on-the-team rather than institutional ("We are looking forward to our journey together"). "You" is reserved for CTAs. Leaders being profiled are written in third person, but the caption typically returns to "we" by its close.

### Emoji as scaffolding

Emojis function structurally, not decoratively. They cluster at the start of bullet info lines (📅 date, 📍 venue, 🔴 live, 🔗 register, ℹ️ know more) and at the end of captions as a CTA block (👇 share thoughts, ➡️ swipe). Almost never mid-sentence.

### Hashtag conventions

Always the final block. Median ~6 tags, maximum ~14. The recurring stack reads as load-bearing: `#MissionToMovement`, `#InvokED2026`, `#EducationLeadership`, `#CollectiveAction`, `#EducationEquity`, `#Shikshagraha`, `#ShikshaLokam`. Programme-specific tags layer in (`#MicroImprovement`, `#DIKSHA`, programme/state names).

### Names and credit

Captions name people fully: name + title + organisation, usually `@`-tagged. Where a title isn't known, the doc carries a literal `(designation)` placeholder rather than guessing. Co-creator stacks credit collaborators at the bottom of carousels ("Pratik Jain | Sushant Kumar | Sonal Bhasin | Sharon Varghese | Ritika Behera"). Honorifics — "Shri" / "Smt." / "Dr." — are used consistently for government officials; "Didi" attaches to one specific leader (Urmila Kumari, Bihar).

### Welcome / hiring templates

Recurring shapes for staff lifecycle posts:

> Welcoming @Bhuvaneshwari Cheboli to ShikshaLokam!
> Joining as Senior Lead – Ecosystem Development, @Shikshagraha…
> Outside of work, she's passionate about farming, sports, and good food.
> We are looking forward to our journey together.
— Welcome post, July 2025

Hiring posts open with a hash-tagged hook then a stacked series of "Do you…?" / "Are you…?" questions.

### How caption voice differs from the blog/web register

- Ed-Leader Portraits **do** lead with a named individual ("This was the reality Rakib Sayyed walked into") — the blog is more cautious about hero leads. Non-portrait captions retain the anti-hero framing ("You don't need to be a superhero to be a leader").
- Question-as-opener is a caption-specific move; the blog reads as discursive essay.
- Captions carry rigid emoji/hashtag scaffolding the blog doesn't have.
- Captions ask explicitly for action ("Share your thoughts in comments," "Register here"); the blog doesn't.
- Pronoun warmth — captions sound on-the-team; the blog/web is more institutional.

### Recurring caption-form vocabulary

Phrases that appear in captions specifically (counts approximate across the doc):

- "What does it take to…" — recurring opener, ~15 verbatim instances
- "micro-improvement(s)" — ~33 instances, often glossed inline ("small, strategic actions / one impactful step at a time")
- "collective action" — ~78; "collective leadership" and "collective ownership" recur
- "Samaaj, Sarkaar, Bazaar, Sanchaar" — invariant brand quartet, every element repeats
- "Mission to Movement" — InvokED 5.0 tagline, also used as a hashtag
- "Sense → Make Sense → Learn → Improve" — the four-step operating model, repeated identically
- "Brain Dates," "Bright Spots" — InvokED session formats that bleed into captions verbatim
- "Ed-Leader Portrait" — both a post-type label and an in-text phrase
- "Up Close & Personal" — the podcast/studio brand; episodes follow a "is coming tomorrow!" → "NOW Live!" cadence

### What captions explicitly avoid

- Marketing superlatives, urgency tropes ("game-changing," "breakthrough," "revolutionary")
- Individual-hero framing in non-portrait posts. The dissemination plan names this directly: *"Frame them as contributors, not content. Maintain respectful, non-promotional tone."*
- Standalone statistic-bombs — numbers are always paired with a human consequence ("1,500+ schools and nearly 69,000 students across five Western Ghats states"; "from 145 to over 600 students")
- Devanagari script — Hindi-origin terms ("Samaaj," "Didi") appear transliterated, never in script

## Story over report — narrative voice for portraits, coffee-book stories, long blogs

Added 2026-06-08 from `learnings/2026-05-27-story-over-report.md` (Sonal's first-round feedback on
the Masthanaiah portrait + Nagaland NLNF story) and `learnings/2026-06-08-narrative-throughline-and-fact-grounding.md`
(her second-round feedback on the same portrait). **Binding for any narrative piece — portraits,
coffee-book stories, long blogs, donor letters.**

**The rule.** Write so the reader feels before they conclude. Five moves:

1. **Build an arc.** Beginning → tension → turn → cost → outcome → close. If every paragraph
   feels the same, it's reporting. The middle should feel different from the turn.
2. **Slow at human moments.** The borrowed television. The tonic-syrup-bottle lamp. The
   teacher who walked two kilometres every day. Give the moment its own paragraph. Don't
   bury it inside a sentence about something else.
3. **Fewer em-dashes. Vary sentence length.** Em-dashes are the punctuation of reporting.
   One per paragraph in narrative, max. Short sentences make rhythm; long sentences make
   thought. Mix them.
4. **Show the cost.** What did this leader give up? When did they doubt themselves? What
   was the hardest moment? A portrait without cost is a CV.
5. **Let the leader feel like a person.** Family. Roots. What they read now. The texture
   institutional vocabulary skips.

**Never** in a narrative piece:

- *"X happened. He did Y. He delivered Z."* — flat reporting cadence; collapse into an arc.
- Stacking three achievements in one paragraph because they fall in the same chapter — each
  beat deserves its own paragraph.
- Institutional verbs (*"originated · delivered · cascaded"*) inside human moments.
- "The X asked them" / "The school was tasked" — demanding voice. Prefer "encouraged" /
  "chose to" / "stepped up to."
- Numbers without a human consequence behind them.

**Always for narrative pieces:**

- A **Gaps that would lift this story** section at the bottom, phrased as questions the
  source-person could answer. Surface what's missing — don't silently smooth over thin
  material. (Sonal: *"Let's ask the AI to highlight gaps that would help create this portrait."*)

### A well-told arc still isn't a story without a throughline (added 2026-06-08)

A v2 Masthanaiah recompile applied all five moves above, beat by beat — and Sonal still called it
fragmented: *"The narrative of education leadership and how it works to change system should be
brought in. (Currently it seems fragmented)... shift from one paragraph to another paragraph should
be smooth which carries on the overall narrative forward... The hook para is missing."* The lesson:
craft per paragraph isn't enough if the paragraphs don't visibly serve **one stated idea**. Four
more moves, layered on top of the five above:

6. **Name the throughline before drafting, then write the hook to state it.** Not "his career" —
   the specific claim the career proves (e.g., "a system changed because the person it nearly
   failed refused to let it fail anyone else"). The hook paragraph states that claim up front, in
   the brain's own voice, before the backstory begins. The close then answers it.
7. **Every paragraph break should carry the throughline forward, not just the chronology.**
   Replace neutral chapter-markers ("Then began the years inside...", "Then, in 1996, he did the
   thing...") with sentences that say *why this chapter is the next expression of the same idea*
   ("That same instinct... travelled with him to Hyderabad"). A transition that would fit any
   leader's biography isn't doing its job.
8. **Diplomatic register in connective tissue, not just human moments.** Default to measured,
   motive-explaining language over combative staging. *"Teacher groups pushed back, he held the
   line"* reads like a fight; *"Teachers' associations resisted the change. He stayed with it,
   because he believed the children were owed the whole day"* keeps the cost and explains the
   *why* without casting anyone as an antagonist. Same lens as "the Fest asked them" → "the Fest
   encouraged them" — prefer language that explains motive over language that stages conflict.
9. **Cross-check every vivid beat against the source before it ships.** If a sentence sounds *too*
   good — a clean image, a clean turn, a clean irony — re-read it against the transcript. Sonal
   caught two: an invented image that didn't even parse (*"The room watched the room"*, not in the
   transcript) and a dramatized causal beat the transcript records quite differently (*"He turned
   around and never paid those fees"*). When the source is genuinely ambiguous, write only the
   part that's clearly supported. Duller and true beats vivid and invented, every time.
