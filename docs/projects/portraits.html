From the fifth-class dropout — forty years of holding the line.

How Shri Vempuluru Nagoor Masthanaiah carried discipline from a village under a tree to Andhra Pradesh's longest-serving Commissioner of School Education.

Role: Recently retired Director, Commissioner of School Education (CSE), Samagra Shiksha, Andhra Pradesh
Location: Origin village on the railway line between Gudur & Renigunta, Tirupati district, Andhra Pradesh. Served across Hyderabad, Medak, Nalgonda, and the AP state HQ.
Lead image: pending — Masthanaiah Sir has agreed to share photos from his career; team to chase.

When the interviewer summarises his journey as from teacher to commissioner, he cuts in: "Not from the teacher — from dropout of fifth class."

In the village where Vempuluru Nagoor Masthanaiah began primary school, the school had no walls. "It's just a small name tree under the tree in the street only," he says. Four classes ran in one room. Lamps were tonic-syrup bottles stuffed with a kerosene wick, the oil rationed by his father. He dropped out twice — first after fifth class, again after seventh. Each time, an outsider pulled him back. First his primary teacher, Sankara Raju, who walked two kilometres each day from the next village and is still the reason the village has any 10th-pass at all. Then a distant relative, a diploma engineer, who took him to Gudur and got him into a Scheduled Tribe hostel for ninth and tenth class. In 1979, fifteen years after the school was founded, Nagoor became the first student it ever produced who passed tenth class. He has not stopped since.

Forty years in Andhra Pradesh's school education system followed. Nine years as a teacher of Science and Mathematics for Classes 6 to 10 — single-handed across three to four batches — in a remote Nellore-district school. He bet on a simple rule: "On the basis of strength enrollment of the students, the other facilities will come." He turned two government upper-primaries into high schools. As Deputy Educational Officer in Hyderabad from 1993, he reformed the morning-afternoon shift system that had hollowed teaching hours, and — in 1996 — originated the counselling-based teacher transfer system that ended decades of pull-driven postings and is now standard practice across departments and states. "Some pressure groups have suffered," he notes, with no apology.

In his final 5.5 years as Director, CSE Andhra Pradesh — the longest tenure anyone has held that cadre post — he co-led the SALT project document for the World Bank, delivered the second-highest number of PM SHRI schools in India for AP (after Uttar Pradesh), and built the School Leadership Development Programme: 250 school heads to IIM Ahmedabad, then a cascade across all leaders of AP's ~45,000 schools, in a profession he had watched receive every form of training except the one for leadership.

"Aims must be good. Aims must be good. If aims are good, results automatically will be good."

Voice notes — what this portrait kept and resisted

How the brain held the voice.

Per the styleguide + the live gallery's register
  • Kept — leader-foregrounded opening, location-rooted, one specific origin moment (the tonic-syrup-bottle lamp), the named first teacher (Sankara Raju), the one-line own-voice close. Matches the live gallery's descriptive arc-titles, not promotional taglines.
  • Resisted — dressing him in language he didn't use. His English is broken, declarative, repetitive; his authority comes from concrete acts, not aphorisms. No Sanskrit-flourish, no "thought leader," no superlatives.
  • Held — the verb count. Originated · reformed · delivered · cascaded. He's a man who built systems; the verbs do the work.
