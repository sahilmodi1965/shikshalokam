Project intent

The container, the themes, the collective.

The Nagaland coffee book sits inside ShikshaLokam's broader Small Steps / Micro Miracles series. As of 26 May 2026, its canonical container is NLNF 3.0 — the Nagaland Literacy & Numeracy Fest 3.0, a state improvement programme inside Nagaland's drive towards NIPUN Nagaland (foundational literacy and numeracy for all, by 2027). The book will celebrate fifteen winning schools under two umbrella themes Sonal named in the brief — collective effort and micro-improvement (series of small steps to achieve an objective).

NLNF 3.0 structure
  • Pre-Primary → Grade 3: five Micro-Improvement Project (MIP) cycles around foundational skills.
  • Grades 4 and 5: Project-Based Learning in language and mathematics — critical thinking, problem-solving, real-world application. (This is the strand the DEP / Nagaland PBL macro story sits inside — 400 teachers, 200 schools across Dimapur and Kiphire.)
  • Structured mentoring layer — workshops, online sessions, one-to-one guidance for teachers and school leaders.
  • Mega PTMs across all grades — parents and communities as active partners.
  • Recognition stack — school-, district-, state-level celebration of teacher, student, parent contributions.
  • Governance + evaluation — accountability and scale.
The collective behind NLNF 3.0
Department of School Education, Nagaland + Samagra Shiksha Nagaland + SCERT Nagaland · ShikshaLokam (project management, micro-improvements design, government liaison, technology) · Mantra4Change (capacity building of education leaders) · NagaED (contextual programme design, on-ground implementation) · Education Above All (content design + funding).

Story 1 · status review-ready · consent: written
Pebbles, poems, and a borrowed television.

How GMS Jalukie Sector-B — one of fifteen NLNF 3.0 winning schools — built foundational learning out of what was already in the village.

Subject: Head Teacher Kuzie Kulim & her four-teacher cohort — Ichaulu (Literacy), Rebiheing (Numeracy), Maheingaule (Pre-Primary)
Location: Government Middle School Jalukie Sector-B, Peren district, Nagaland
Lead image: pending — 4 teacher profiles + 2 school exteriors + 8 implementation photos all consented & uploaded; editorial pick TBD

In a school where the network drops, the power flickers, and most parents are daily-wage earners, foundational learning began the day the Head Teacher carried in a television.

In Peren district, the road between schools is hilly, the network is uncertain, and the electricity is not promised. At Government Middle School Jalukie Sector-B, most students are the first in their families to attend school. When the rains come, attendance falls — children are needed at home, on the land, with the cattle. This is the school that, in 2026, won its way into the fifteen.

The Fest asked them to do things they had never done. Record videos. Upload them through a network that wouldn't hold. "Uploading project videos often took a long time," the school writes. "Despite these difficulties, we tried our best to complete and submit all project work on time." When the pre-primary classroom needed audio-visual learning — and the school had no television — Head Teacher Kuzie Kulim arranged one "so that students studying in government schools would not feel disadvantaged compared to others."

Then the small steps began.

Literacy teacher Ichaulu introduced a poem and read it aloud, line by line. Children recited with her — with actions, with expression — every week from Term 1 onwards. Numeracy teacher Rebiheing picked up the everyday objects of the classroom — and asked children to arrange them by size, by weight, by number. Heavy and light. Heaviest and lightest. Pre-primary teacher Maheingaule opened her bag to what the village already had: pebbles, twigs, leaves, building blocks, visuals. Children, she writes, are "more enthusiastic and eager to learn compared to conventional method."

Parents — many of whom had never been to school themselves — were uneasy at first. The school called Parent-Teacher Meetings, explained the purpose, and the unease became participation.

"Quality foundational learning is possible even with limited resources." — the school, in its own words.

Reading, pronunciation, vocabulary, comprehension all up in the literacy class. Size, weight, ordering — and confidence — up in numeracy. Communication, social interaction, creativity, and joy up in pre-primary. Class participation went from quiet to active across all three.

"With creativity, teamwork and community support, students can become active, confident and enthusiastic learners."

What they will keep doing: Activity-based learning · play-based learning · storytelling · poem recitation with actions · locally-available teaching-learning materials · regular Parent-Teacher Meetings.

  • Story-first, data after. The reader meets Peren, the borrowed television, and Kuzie Kulim before meeting micro-improvement as a term. The term arrives the way it earns itself — as a series of small, named acts (poem, pebbles, PTM).
