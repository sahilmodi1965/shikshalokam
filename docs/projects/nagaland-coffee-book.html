Project intent

The Nagaland coffee book sits inside ShikshaLokam's broader Small Steps / Micro Miracles series — visual-first volumes that carry stories of teachers, ed-leaders, communities, and students who moved one specific outcome in one specific place. Distinct from the Brazil presentation (slide format) and the SLC blogs (long-form first-person) — coffee books carry short, image-paired vignettes (≤300 words per story).

Canonical container (as of 2026-05-26): Nagaland Literacy & Numeracy Fest (NLNF) 3.0 — the state's drive towards NIPUN Nagaland (foundational literacy and numeracy for all by 2027). This book celebrates 15 NLNF 3.0 winning schools under two umbrella themes Sonal named in her brief: collective effort and micro-improvement (series of small steps to achieve an objective).

NLNF 3.0 structure (see programs § NLNF):

  • Pre-Primary → Grade 3 — five MIP cycles around foundational skills.
  • Grades 4 and 5 — Project-Based Learning in language and mathematics (this is the strand the DEP / Nagaland PBL macro story sits inside — 400 teachers, 200 schools across Dimapur and Kiphire; see shikshalokam-brazil-stories-may2026 § Nagaland).
  • Structured mentoring · Mega PTMs · school/district/state recognition stack · governance + evaluation.

The collective behind NLNF 3.0: Department of School Education + Samagra Shiksha Nagaland + SCERT Nagaland · ShikshaLokam (project management, micro-improvements design, government liaison, technology) · Mantra4Change · NagaED · Education Above All (content + funding).

Story — Pebbles, poems, and a borrowed television
  • Status: review-ready (v2 — recompiled 2026-05-27 after Sonal's structural feedback; v1 archived in chatlog)
  • Subject: A four-teacher cohort led by Head Teacher Kuzie Kulim, with Ichaulu (Literacy), Rebiheing (Numeracy), and Maheingaule (Pre-Primary).
  • Location: Government Middle School Jalukie Sector-B, Peren district, Nagaland.
  • Lead image: Pending. Four teacher profile photos, two school exteriors, and eight project-implementation photos are all consented and uploaded to Drive (see nlnf3-gms-jalukie-sectorb-response-2026-05-07 § Image assets). Editorial pick TBD.
  • Title (working): Pebbles, poems, and a borrowed television.
  • Subtitle (working): How a four-teacher cohort at GMS Jalukie Sector-B — in a corner of Peren district where the network drops and the power flickers — turned what they had into how their children learn to read and count.
  • Hook (1 line): In a school where most parents are daily-wage earners and the nearest reliable signal is a long walk away, foundational learning began the day the head teacher carried in a television.
  • Body (~600 words, narrative arc, fewer en-dashes):

In Peren district, the road between schools is hilly. The mobile network drops without warning. The electricity is not promised. In the season of rain, children stay home — there is work waiting at home, on the small piece of land, with the cattle, and the family is fed by what the children's hands also do.

At Government Middle School Jalukie Sector-B, most of the students are the first in their families ever to attend school. Most of the parents have never been to school themselves. Many earn by the day. Teachers who arrived to teach reading and counting found they were also teaching a new idea — that going to school every day, all year, was a thing worth doing at all.

In 2026, this small school — out of every government school in the state of Nagaland — became one of fifteen winners of the Nagaland Literacy and Numeracy Fest 3.0. The Fest is the state's drive towards NIPUN Nagaland: foundational literacy and numeracy for every child by 2027. The Fest is built on a simple idea. Children learn to read and count not through one big change, but through many small ones, again and again, across the year. The teachers at Jalukie Sector-B decided to try.

The Fest encouraged them to do things the school had never been asked to do before. Record videos of their classroom work. Upload them on a network that often would not hold. "Uploading project videos often took a long time," the school writes, in the kind of plain sentence that hides what it actually took. "Despite these difficulties, we tried our best to complete and submit all project work on time."

Then one day the pre-primary classroom needed audio-visual learning. The school had no television.

Head Teacher Kuzie Kulim went out into the village and arranged one. Not because anyone asked her to. Because, in her words, "students studying in government schools would not feel disadvantaged compared to others."

Of every line in the response form the school filled out, that is the one we want the reader to carry. A head teacher in a remote district, in a school with hard rain and harder networks, decided that her children would not feel like second-class learners. So she found a television.

Then the small steps began.

In the literacy classroom, Ichaulu opened with a poem. She read it aloud, one line at a time, her own voice teaching the pronunciation. Then she asked the children to recite it with her — with actions, with expression — week after week from Term 1. By the time the term ended, the children who had once been silent were reciting the poem in chorus, with the actions, all of them, together.

In the numeracy classroom, Rebiheing reached for what the room already had. Stones. Bottle caps. Bits of stick. She asked the children to sort them by size. Then by weight. Then by number. Heaviest and lightest. Heavy and light. The classroom became something the children recognised — the way they sort what they help with at home.

In the pre-primary classroom, Maheingaule opened her bag to pebbles, twigs, leaves, building blocks, simple pictures. Children, she writes, are "more enthusiastic and eager to learn compared to conventional method." It is a quiet sentence. It is also the sound of a teacher realising that play is not a break from learning, it is the way it starts.

The parents, at first, were uneasy. They did not recognise this kind of teaching from their own childhoods. The school called a Parent-Teacher Meeting and explained, slowly, what the children were doing in the new classrooms and why. After the meeting, the unease became participation. Parents began asking questions. Then they began coming back.

When the school was asked what they now believed was possible — having put themselves through one full cycle of the Fest, with all the things that had been hard about it — they wrote a single line.

*"Quality foundational learning is possible even with limited resources. With creativity, teamwork and community support, students can become active, confident and enthusiastic learners."*

This is the line. This is the story.

This is the lesson the winning schools of NLNF 3.0 are quietly carrying back to the rest of Nagaland. You do not begin with what you do not have. You begin with what is in the village already. The pebbles. The poems. And, when the children deserve it, a television borrowed from a neighbour.

  • What they will keep doing, after the Fest: Activity-based learning. Play-based learning. Storytelling and poem recitation with actions. Locally-available teaching-learning materials. Regular Parent-Teacher Meetings. "Because they have helped students become more active, confident, creative and enthusiastic learners."

Voice notes — what this v2 changed

  • From reporting to story. v1 stacked challenges + steps + outcome in one rush. v2 builds a small arc: the place → the Fest's invitation → the borrowed-television moment as the emotional centre → the small steps in each classroom → the parents turning around → the single line the school wrote about what is possible.
  • Empathy + context up front. v1 said "this is the school that, in 2026, won its way into the fifteen" without explaining what it won, what NLNF 3.0 is, or why fifteen matters. v2 opens with the place and the parents first, then names the Fest, what it means, why the children showed up despite the rain.
  • "Encouraged," not "asked." Per Sonal's note. The Fest is not a demanding voice in the school's life; it is an invitation the teachers chose to accept.
  • The borrowed-television moment gets its own beat. v1 buried it inside the challenges paragraph. v2 gives it three paragraphs of weight — Kuzie Kulim's reason in her own words, followed by one paragraph of pure reflection on what that decision actually means.
  • Far fewer em-dashes. v1 had 14 em-dashes; v2 has ~4, all of them earning their place.

Gaps that would lift this story (for Sonal to chase via Aquib / NagaED / the school)
